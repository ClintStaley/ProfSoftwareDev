Writing Dings


Overview

The following list of common errors may help you to remember what to look for when critiquing technical writing, and to avoid making these mistakes in your own writing. It's not a comprehensive list, but perhaps 90% of technical writing errors can be put in one of these categories. Sometimes looking for what's wrong helps you get it right.


Since you'll be asked to mark documents with errors, the list includes a two-letter abbreviation for each type of error. Consider these a standard shorthand to use when editing.


Sequencing Errors

PR Prereq assumed incorrectly, or without supporting reference

WS Wrong sequence. Idea X should have gone before idea Y

MS Missing Step. Idea X, an important step, is missing.


Ambiguity Errors

AP Ambiguous pronoun

WC Poor word choice. Use word W instead (specify)

CO Poor clause order

EX Concept explained incompletely or insufficiently

IN Inaccurate statement


Terminology Errors

VC Use of undefined vocabulary/jargon

IT Inconsistent use of terms

CT Need to coin a term


Abstract/concrete errors

CR Needs a concrete example

AC Abstraction placed before concrete

AB Needlessly abstract phrasing – be more direct

BP Reiterating code or steps without showing big picture


Pictorial

DG Needs a diagram

BD Bad diagram – needs improvement (specify)


General writing

WD Too wordy

UW Useless words added (special case of WD)

PV Passive voice could be made active

FM Bad formatting, line breaks, vertical spacing

BG Bad Grammar

SP Misspelling




Exercise

Look for a Wikipedia topic on a technical area with which you are familiar. Pick a subject where clear tutorial explanation would be of value (such as the insertion sort we did, though it need not be a 101/102 topic), and where the existing Wikipedia article could be significantly improved, using the approaches we've discussed.


1. Find an article, and identify a page of it as a rewrite subject. Be sure it meets the following criteria:

i. Article must be on a reasonably sophisticated STEM subject, but not necessarily CSC. (e.g insertion sort, resistor networks, collision kinematics, etc.)

ii. Article must be about one page long, or have an independently editable section or set of sections totalling about one page.

iii. Existing prose must be poorly enough written to provide ample opportunity for "dings" and improvement.

iv. Topic must not be so prominent as to draw flames for being modified

v. People in class must know enough about the topic to explain it well.


2. Critique the article, using the "dings" list we had in class. Snapshot an HTML copy of the page (they change a lot so I want the one you critiqued) and your dings marked right on the copy, in <b> tags, surrounded by []. Find at least 30 dings, but then cull this list down to at most 15 dings, and be sure you can clearly defend each one – don't keep dings that are margin calls.


3. Email your edited article to me and the class, with Email title “Wikipedia Edit <your name>”. We'll examine these in class and lab, and we'll update the Wikipedia article right there based on class feedback. The more dings of yours actually make it into the article with class agreement, the better your score :)
