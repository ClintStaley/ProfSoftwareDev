Writing vs Speaking

Writing is a more demanding medium than speaking. We are wired to speak and listen – humanity has been doing it for tens of thousands of years. We're not as instinctively good and writing and reading, since most of humanity has been doing those for only a couple of centuries. Things you can get away with in spoken form will not work in written form, because writing lacks the additional bandwidth of tone, cadence, and facial expression, and the general forgiveness we offer as listeners vs readers. (If you doubt that latter, try reading a literal transcript of a spoken conversation – it will sound awkward, ambiguous, and ill phrased, even it it sounded fine in spoken form.)

But, of course, writing has advantages. Written material is available to the student at any time, and the reader can absorb the material at his own pace, going slower or faster, and rereading as needed. The opportunity to read quickly is a particular plus. Most people can read much faster than anyone can speak, and indeed even faster than they could listen to sped-up speech. In computer terms, written material offers the highest input bandwidth. And if the document is well organized, the reader can use it in random-access mode, jumping directly to points of interest.

As we'll discuss in a later lecture, online podcasts offer some of these advantages, but even they cannot match the random-access of a written work and its high input bandwidth.

So, with particular attention to STEM writing, how do we work around the disadvantages of written communication in order to get those advantages?

Avoid Ambiguity

“Avoid ambiguity” is always good advice, of course. But here are some particular points to watch for in STEM writing.

Watch out for Pronouns

Pronouns like “it”, “that”, etc invite trouble. Technical prose is “noun rich” -- it usually discusses several things at the same time, and pronouns are thus usually ambiguous. An example:

“In this linked list algorithm, we advance the pointer temp from the first node to the second, and then check to see if it has nonnegative data.”

What does “it” refer to in that sentence? “It” could be the first node or the second. Even if the reader can guess which you mean, they're left with a nagging feeling that they might have been wrong. Unless a pronoun is clearly unambiguous, replace it with the noun to which it refers: “.. to see if the second node has nonnegative data”.

Word Choice

Seemingly unimportant shifts in choice of wording can make a big difference in clarity, again more so in written than in spoken communication. And, as with avoiding ambiguous pronouns, even if the reader can figure out what you mean, it's still more restful and confidence-engendering for them if you are totally clear. Some examples:

“Use malloc to allocate a pointer to a block of data” vs “Use malloc to allocate a block of data.”

The second is correct. Malloc doesn't allocate a pointer. It allocates the data to which the pointer refers.

“We shift the bits down by three positions” vs “We shift the bits to the right by three positions”

The second again is better. “Down” is almost a synonym for “over” in this context – it doesn't imply a clear direction. “To the right” leaves no ambiguity, assuming you're drawing the bits in the usual way.

“We move the value from i to j” vs “We copy the value from i to j”

Moving implies somehow that the value is no longer in i, but of course it's in both i and j. The second form makes that clearer.

Phrase Order

Watch the order in which you place several prepositional phrases or other modifying clauses. Cases in which a modifying phrase might apply to two different subjects often occur in normal writing, and modest instances are acceptable because the intent is almost always clear from context. Not so in STEM prose, where the concepts are more complex, and multiple interpretations are possible. For instance:

“Stop the thread in the process that was running”

Did you mean

“Stop the thread that was running in the process”, or “Stop the thread in the running process”?

Here's another:

“The algorithm prints the last variable in the array that was entered.”

Hmm. Did you mean:

“The algorithm prints the last variable that was entered in the array”, or “From the array that was entered, the algorithm prints the last variable”?

There's always some way to combine the phrases unambiguously, even if it takes two sentences. Be sure to do this.

Conciseness

As the old saying goes, “Brevity is the soul of wit.” Conciseness and elegance are the mark of good STEM writing (and of good STEM work in general). There's no exact rule for how to do this, of course. But, reviewing what you've written to tighten it up is the most important step -- see Revise and Edit below.

Nonconcise example:

A for-loop is a loop that is like a while loop, but also has two added parts in the for-loop header to go along with the loop test. The for-loop header has the following form:

for (initializer; loop test; incrementer)
   loop body;

The first added part, the initializer, is performed one time before the loop starts, and the second added part, the incrementer, lets you do an increment of a loop variable or other action after each of the loop's iterations.

The loop test works just like the loop test in a while-loop; the for-loop continues to run as long as the test is true. But, the initializer, despite being right next to the loop test, is run only once, before the loop starts. The incrementer is done each time the loop runs, though conceptually it is done at the bottom of the loop body, as essentially a final step in the loop body before returning to the top of the loop. (This can be a little confusing since the incrementer is written at the top of the loop in the header, but it's actually done at the bottom of the loop.) If you were to rewrite a for-loop as a while loop instead, this is what you'd do:

initializer;
while (loop test) {
   loop body;
   incrementer;
}

Concise example:

A for-loop works like a while-loop, but with two added parts to its header:

for (initializer; loop test; incrementer)
   loop body;

Like a while-loop, the for-loop continues to run as long as its loop test is true. But, the initializer runs just once, before the loop starts. And, even though it's written at the top of the loop, the incrementer actually runs at the end of each loop iteration. (Don't be confused by its location – it runs at the end of each iteration.) The for-loop is thus equivalent to this while-loop:



initializer;
while (loop test) {
   loop body;
   incrementer;
}

The nonconcise example comprises 202 words; the concise one has 85 words.

Enthusiasm Adverbs

A non-concise writing habit peculiar to engineers is the use of what might be termed enthusiasm adverbs: “basically”, “fundamentally”, “essentially”, and the like. For instance:

“Basically, a linked list is a chain of nodes. Traversing the list is essentially a matter of moving a pointer from node to node down the list.”

The “basically” and “essentially” add no meaning, and perhaps worse, they make the writer sound like a breathlessly enthusiastic geek. Drop them.

Passive Voice

Passive voice sentences, for those not into grammar, are sentences framed without a clear subject – just a verb and direct object. For instance:

The array is traversed until the desired element is found.

The head pointer is initialized to null.

While “array”, “element”, and “head pointer” appear at the start of these sentences or phrases, they are not subjects – not the doers of the action. They're actually direct objects – things upon which the action is done. The subject is omitted altogether, as though the traversing, finding, and initializing are just magically happening.

English allows passive voice structure so that you can describe subjectless actions, and it is sometimes useful. As a humorous example, it's great for avoiding blame: “The package was dropped and the item was broken.” (No particular person did this, of course...)

But, engineers and bureaucrats are notorious for overusing passive voice, perhaps because they feel it sounds formal and proper. The sentence structure has two major drawbacks. First, it's boring, especially if used in sentence after sentence. Second, it communicates less information since it lacks a clear subject. Consider these active voice rewrites of the sentences above:

The first loop traverses the array until the if-statement finds the desired element.

The declaration initializes the head pointer to null.

They convey more information, and are more “lively” and readable. Passive voice is not always wrong; sometimes there just is no clear subject. But before you use it, always try first to frame the sentence in active voice if you reasonably can.

Revise and Edit

There's a legend that Abraham Lincoln wrote his incomparable Gettysburg Address on the back of an envelope on the way to the event. Like many Lincoln legends, this is apocryphal. Lincoln edited and revised the speech intensively before delivering it. Five different versions written in his hand survive to this day. (If you love both history and writing, you should check these out online.)

STEM writing is not great political oratory, but it benefits from revision just as much. Almost no one writes clearly and elegantly in a first draft. In a sense, one might say that good writing is good revision. The writing you give your students should never be your first draft, but rather your Nth draft, after you've considered different phrase orders, word choices, sequencing, etc, and after you've removed extraneous words and tightened your prose.

It's not the first draft that counts; it's the last.
